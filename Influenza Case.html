Pandemic Influenza – Just-in-Time vs. Just-in-Case Strategies1


“It’s kind of odd that the government would expect all those years for us to continue to throw millions of doses of vaccine away every year and never take a hit on it.”[19] “We are a for-profit, publicly traded company. What I like to tell people is that neither Santa Claus nor UNICEF is listed on the NYSE.” – Anonymous Executives in Vaccine Industry [16]


  1. Starting Story

    On a beautiful sunny day in October 2009, Michael Johnson, the vice-president of manufacturing at CSP Corporation’s influenza vaccine division, is planning for the 2010-2011 flu season for the

    U.S. market. Michael’s job is to come up with a production plan that optimizes the company’s bottom-line and meanwhile promotes public health.

    Having been in this industry for more than 20 years, Michael knows the most predictable element of the influenza vaccine market is that it is always going to be different. While the chance of a moderate or severe flu pandemic is very small, its impact on human life and the economy would be devastating. With a life-cycle of only three months (October through December in any year), selling flu vaccine is much like selling turkeys the week before Thanksgiving – some stores will surely throw away turkeys on Friday, but they have already made enough on turkeys sold, and don’t want to run out. However, unlike turkeys for Thanksgiving, influenza vaccine has always been a very cheap product, and the sales may not cover the company’s losses if too many vaccines go unused and must be thrown out.

    While Michael feels that he must make a conservative plan for the best interest of the company, he knows that in case of even a moderate flu pandemic, the supply chain of flu vaccine under such a plan is sure to break down. Demand may double or even triple over its normal size. The thin stockpiles at all levels will soon run out. Vaccine manufacturers won’t be able to resupply in

    6 months. Truck routes could be blocked and borders may be closed, particularly perilous at a time when 80% of raw materials for U.S. drugs come from abroad. In the absence of medicine, hundreds of thousands of people could lose their life while millions could be hospitalized. It is a painful decision to make: just-in-time supply with no just-in-case preparation.

  2. Background

    • Influenza symptoms

      Every year millions of Americans come down with flu during the flu season, which typically starts in November and lasts till April of next year. Influenza virus infects humans with flu symptoms, which cause acute respiratory illness as well as severe headache, fatigue and general discomfort. Strains of influenza virus may experience mutation in their genome over time, and thus every year different strains of influenza virus may affect the U.S. and world population.

    • Flu death toll

      Seasonal flu should be taken seriously and not be dismissed as a “non lethal disease.” Globally, annual outbreaks result in 250,000 to 500,000 deaths [7], and on average, 41,400 people died each year in the United States between 1979 and 2001 from influenza (Wikipedia). Influenza especially poses grave danger to infants and seniors over the age of 65. About 9 out of 10 of those deaths are among people older than 65 [24].

    • Flu pandemic history

      Influenza pandemic is much more devastating than seasonal flu to the human society, see Exhibit 1 for estimates of the damage. In the “Spanish flu” pandemic of 1918 and 1919, more than 500,000 people died in the United States and some 50 million perished worldwide (Wikipedia). The 2009 swine flu pandemic experienced in the United States and the world was due to a novel strain of the influenza virus – the influenza A/H1N1, which began in the spring of 2009. The virus had spread to the US from an outbreak in Mexico [4]. As of mid-March 2010, the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) estimated that about 59 million Americans contracted the H1N1 virus, 265,000 were hospitalized and 12,000 died [21]. Exhibit 2 shows the major flu pandemics records.

    • Social cost of flu

      Influenza extols great misery on people, not only in human lives but also in monetary terms. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), the cost of health care, lost days of work and education and social disruption caused by the flu virus totaled $1-6 million per 100,000 people in the industrialized nation [11]. The cost resulted from flu among civilian employees, which include private industry and state and local government bodies averaged $29.40 per hour

      worked [1]. By a Congressional Budget Office report, the possible price tag of a national but "mild" pandemic that sickens 75 million and kills 100,000 people, akin to the 1957 and 1968 influenza outbreaks, is predicted to be 1 percent drop in GDP [26]. The economic impact of a severe pandemic, estimated to be a nearly $700 billion hit to the U.S. economy, or about a 5% decline in economic output during a one-year span, would be equal to a medium-sized recession [31].

    • Flu vaccine

      Vaccine is the principal tool to combat influenza. A vaccine is a biological preparation that improves immunity to a particular disease. A vaccine typically contains an agent that resembles a disease-causing microorganism, and is often made from weakened or killed forms of the microbe or its toxins. The agent stimulates the body's immune system to recognize the agent as foreign, destroy it, and "recognize" it, so that the immune system can more easily recognize and destroy any of these microorganisms that it later encounters (Wikipedia). For flu, the most popular type of vaccine is the trivalent inactivated influenza vaccines (TIVs), which can handle three different flu viruses and are licensed for use in all age groups as well as for people with chronic medical conditions.

    • Economic impact of flu vaccine

      In the U.S., immunizations in the elderly save $117 per person in medical costs. Systematic vaccination of children can result in significant population-wide benefits [11]. Thus vaccination could be seen as a social benefit from a societal perspective. According to the CDC, in 2009 the US market for flu vaccines totaled $1 billion, and is projected to grow to $3 billion by 2016 [23]. Pediatric vaccines have traditionally been a growth source for the flu vaccine segment, but with the baby boomer generation, senior adults will likely take over this position in the coming years.

    • Stakeholders in influenza vaccines

      Many parties are involved in the flu vaccine value chain: Government, manufacturers, distributors, retail outlets and health-care providers. Their roles are described in Exhibit 3.

  3. Manufacturing Timeline

    • Flu virus shifting and vaccine shelf-life

      The influenza virus evolves rapidly, and new strains quickly replace the older ones. The influenza vaccine is reformulated each year in an attempt to match the strain(s) of the virus anticipated to circulate in the coming season. Because it is reformulated annually, the influenza vaccine has a limited shelf life – its functional shelf-life is only a few months (typically, October through December in a given year).

    • Timeline overview

      Manufacturers of influenza vaccines follow an annual process of planning and production as follows. Producing influenza vaccine is a complex process that involves growing viruses in millions of fertilized chicken eggs. This process, which requires several steps, generally takes 6 to 8 months from January through August each year, so vaccine manufacturers must estimate vaccine demand and decide the number of eggs needed for the next season in advance well before the onset of the flu season. The timeline of the production process can be broken down into the following distinct stages for the flu season 2010-2011:

    • Stage 0 - Securing hen eggs for vaccine production (late October 2009 till January 2010)

      In late October 2009, as many as 100,000 adolescent chicks are raised for three months to mature hens on many farms. Egg-laying begins in late January 2010, and typically one egg per day is collected. These special and treated eggs are then incubated till day 11 and fertilized eggs are then shipped to the manufacturing facilities.

    • Stage 1- Selection of virus strain and demand forecasting (December 2009 to January 2010)

      During the winter season (December 2009 to January 2010), the CDC through collaboration with healthcare experts all around the world, helps to predict which strain of flu will be prevalent in the U.S. for the following season. The viruses used in making seasonal flu vaccine are chosen based on information gathered over the previous year about the strains of flu viruses that are infecting humans and how they are changing. The data about the disease trends are gathered from 122 national influenza centers in 94 countries, and the viruses for the following fall season are chosen [6]. These selected strains of virus are then handed over to manufacturers to produce vaccines. Even with all the efforts, the selection of the target strains is not perfect due to antigenic drift, which may require influenza vaccines to be reformulated during the flu season. The swine flu presents an example. The CDC also works with all manufacturers each year to secure the necessary supply and project the total amount to be produced.

    • Stage 2 – Harvesting of virus from egg (from February 2010 to May 2011)

      The vaccine production process starts around mid February, with injecting the live virus obtained from the CDC into those 11-day old fertilized egg. A typical trivalent vaccine (vaccine made of three virus strains) contains two subtypes of influenza A (influenza A is commonly called H1N1 and H3N2) and one of influenza B [6]. One reason for adding multiple virus strains to vaccines is to minimize the risk if wrong strains of virus are selected. Another reason is that the subtype of influenza A, virus strain H3N2, is very lethal, so for good common practice, trivalent vaccines in the U.S. always contain this virus strain. The virus injected into the egg then multiplies as the embryo develops, and the virus-loaded fluid is harvested from the allantonic fluid of the egg around mid May.

    • Stage 3- Purification, Quality Control and inspection of Vaccine (from June 2010 till July 2010)

      The live virus harvested from the egg fluid is then subjected to several procedures and processes to purify and to make it chemically inactive in order to make the vaccine safe for human use. To immunize people against flu virus, influenza vaccines require a single injection of 15 μg haemagglutinin (antigen) [8]. The manufacturer, along with the Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research (CBER), a part of the Food and Drug Administration (FDA), screens the vaccine for contamination issues, quality concerns and for appropriate potency testing. Quality control for appropriate potency starts at the manufacturer once the FDA provides the reference reagents. The flu vaccine is compared against the reference reagents to confirm that the concentration of the hemagglutinin antigen is correct.

    • Stage 4- Filling and Packaging (August 2010)

      Once the FDA releases vaccine lots for immunization purpose, the manufacturer quickly completes the process of filling, labeling and packaging. The lot release process must be repeated for each lot of vaccine the manufacturer produces. This step commences the production process in the vaccine supply chain and initiates the start of the distribution process.

    • Stage 5- Distribution of Flu Vaccine (September 2010 till March 2011)

      The produced vaccine is then shipped and distributed to pharmaceutical distributors, physician offices, hospitals, pharmacies, health care clinics, nursing homes and local and regional government. By November 2010 and before the start of the peak flu season, the first batches of flu vaccines are shipped to required locations.

      This is the yearly cycle for the northern hemisphere. The southern hemisphere uses a separate cycle. Globally, within the two production cycles, almost 250 million doses are delivered to over 100 countries per year. Exhibit 4 summarizes the annual production cycle of vaccine for the northern hemisphere.

  4. Demand Characteristics

    • Uncertain demand, prediction factors

      One of most significant challenges for vaccine manufacturers is to predict the demand. The United States has seen volatile fluctuations in demand over the past decade. While the market continues to expand and demand continues to increase, the exact amount required is hardly predictable because demand for the vaccine depends to some extent on unpredictable features of the influenza season, such as the timing and severity of outbreaks.

      The most important demand factors are: (1) the target population for vaccination who are especially vulnerable to influenza-associated complications, (2) demographics (race, ethnicity, socioeconomic status) in the United States, determining the size of the target population; (3) the likelihood of a healthy individual obtaining the vaccine to protect family members and minimize work loss and suffering due to influenza illness. While the first two factors are straightforward statistics, the third one depends on public perception of the severity of the flu season.

      If the public perceives that the influenza strain is mild or if outbreaks of the disease do not occur until January or later, demand tends to fall. Further, if the prior season was mild and if many individuals who went without influenza vaccine in the prior season remained healthy, demand in the current season is likely to be low. For instance, in the 2008-2009 flu season, less than 40% of adults (age 18 and older) were vaccinated. A survey shows that among those who were not vaccinated, the primary reasons are “don’t need it” (25%), “didn’t get around to it” (16%), “don’t believe in it” (15%) and “might get sick/side effects” (15%) [15]. This is typical in a “good” year when the flu season isn’t severe and many people choose not to get vaccinated and take advantage of the “free riders” if vaccination rates for others ensure herd immunity.

      However, in years when influenza is perceived to be severe or occurs early (around November or December), demand for flu vaccines is likely to increase. For example, in the 2003–2004 influenza season, worries started in Colorado when the influenza season began early and deaths were reported among children, suggesting an unusually virulent illness. In early November, the

      first reports of high absenteeism in schools were made available. By the end of the month, Colorado’s vaccine locator Web site had 2.3 million hits. In December, the Web site received 2.9 million hits. In January, the demand began to diminish rapidly. A health official explained,


      “… 2003–2004 … was the first year we used all of the [influenza] vaccine. We never [had] used all of the influenza vaccine supply until that year, …”

      CDC ultimately described the 2003–2004 season as “moderately severe”. By May 31, 2004, a total of 152 influenza-associated deaths occurred among those 18 years of age or younger [18].     • Recommended recipients

      Before 2000, the Advisory Committee on Immunization Practice (ACIP) recommended flu vaccination primarily of elderly and other high-risk people (e.g., healthcare providers). In 2000, ACIP broadened its initial target group – persons 65 years of age or older and persons with health conditions that would place them at particular risk for influenza-related complications – to include all persons 50 years of age or older regardless of health status. In 2004, the recommendations broadened again to include young children 6 to 23 months of age [18]. See Exhibit 5 for more details.

      In the United States, the number of people in priority target groups for the vaccine in 2004–2005 was 88 million, and the number in additional target groups (e.g., health care workers) was 95 million in 2005 [16]. The industry commonly believes that about 80 million doses of influenza vaccine are demanded at least in a typical year, regardless of the fluctuations. Even if everyone in targeted groups were to seek out the influenza vaccine, the number of doses demanded would

      be approximately 183.3 million per year, or 62 percent of the estimated total U.S. population of

      297.2 million in 2005 [18]. This number includes persons 65 years of age or older, those with defined chronic diseases, pregnant women, children 6 to 23 months of age, household contacts of those previously mentioned, health care personnel, and healthy persons 50 to 64 years of age. At the onset of a flu pandemic, the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) targets at covering the entire population aged 6 month and older (e.g., around 290 million in 2005) within six months [3].

    • Actual uptakes

      Actual uptake remained unpredictable but typically less than 50% of the recommended population [12]. For example, in 2002, although more than 90 percent of children 19 to 35 months of age received recommended childhood vaccines (which did not include influenza vaccine), only 66 percent of people 65 years of age or older received an influenza vaccine [18].   5. Manufacturing Risks

    Despite the millions of doses wasted each year, the U.S. frequently experiences shortages of flu vaccines. The shortages mean that many people who want a “flu shot” are unable to get one. The shortages are not only due to conservative production of manufacturers, but also due to various risks faced by manufacturers.

    • Production risk

      In addition to uncertain demand, vaccine manufacturers have to fight with many other challenges that expose them to potential risks. First, the egg process requires a long production cycle of 6-8 months, and if anything goes wrong with the production process, the entire batch of vaccine is affected. Since flu vaccines are produced and consumed only once every year, any glitches in the production process would mean an entire breakdown of the total vaccine supply chain.

      The complexities of producing influenza vaccine, coupled with the small number of influenza vaccine manufacturers, make it nearly impossible to produce more vaccine than originally planned in advance. Therefore, one manufacturer cannot make up another’s shortfall when vaccine is lost or delayed. Consequently, if one manufacturer encounters a production problem, overall shortage is likely to happen.

    • Random yield

      Manufacturers also face random yield – the number of viral particles generated by an egg that could be used to make the vaccine is random, which makes the output of vaccine highly uncertain. The data on yield is highly controversial. According to Chiron's executive director Bryett, “If you're very lucky, you'll get three doses per egg.” However, if you are not lucky, you may need three eggs to produce a single dose of seasonal flu vaccine [13]. Yet another source states: 143-146 million doses expected for the 2008-09 season and 132-133 million eggs required to produce vaccine for the 2008-09 season [10]. As a rule of thumb, two eggs are needed to produce one individual shot of the flu vaccine [29].

    • Tort liability

      Flu vaccine manufacturers have to face the potentially significant compensation of personal injury liability (called tort liability). Tort liability has been a severe risk for vaccines relative to other drugs because vaccines treat healthy people, usually children and the elderly. For instance, most recent claims are related to thimerosal, a mercury-based preservative used in several pediatric vaccines [12].

    • Recent examples

      The supply of seasonal-influenza vaccines has been disrupted several times by production problems in the recent past. In 2000–2001, manufacturing problems at two companies (out of a total of three) resulted in lower than expected yields, causing delays in delivery of vaccine that created shortages. In 2001–2002, spot shortages were reported in some areas early in the season and were more widespread in 2003–2004. In 2004–2005, the US-based Chiron Corporation was supposed to supply 46 to 48 million doses for the U.S. market. However, U.K. regulators suspended production at Chiron’s Liverpool, England plant after the company failed to resolve contamination issues. Aventis-Pasteur, the other vaccine supplier, was only able to increase its production from 54 million doses to 58 million. As a result, the U.S. lost nearly half of the 100 million vaccine doses planned for the 2004-2005 influenza season and had to resort to stringent rationing of immunization for the most needed [30]. Exhibit 8 summarizes some of the key issues occurring during these six influenza seasons prior to 2006. Exhibit 12 shows the rationing policy in case of shortage.

  6. Manufacturing Economics

    • Cost structure

      Vaccine production tends to be highly capital-intensive and therefore the fixed operating cost is high. A new vaccine facility in the U.S. has been reported to cost $150 million for large-scale egg-based (100 million doses/season) production [27]. According to a government body source [3], Sanofi Aventis spent $150 million on a new facility in order to triple its current production capacity of 50 million (doses/season) to 150 million (doses/season). New production facilities may take 5 to 7 years to build, and are highly specialized [16].

      By [28], quoting work by Mercer Management Consulting Co., fixed production costs excluding R&D represent 60% of total vaccine production costs. Costs that are essentially fixed per batch amount to roughly 25% of total costs. Of this, the biggest fixed cost per batch is eggs. Eggs account for approximately 50% of bulk vaccine cost in inactivated influenza vaccine manufacturing. The size of a batch is 1000-liter. Fully variable costs are on the order of 15% [28].

    • Pricing & cost

      Between 1999 and 2006, list prices (which are set by manufacturers and reported to the CDC) for influenza vaccine jumped from about $2 to about $11 per dose. In general, individual clinicians, clinics, and hospitals pay list price or close to it; large groups, state consortia, and health plans often can negotiate for rebates and discounts (Institute of Medicine 2004, pp. 127–128).

      In the 2009-2010 season, vaccine cost per dose for the manufacturer is about $3 dollars, not including the depreciated costs for the capital required to invest in the manufacturing facility. The fixed costs related to quality assurance, administration and depreciation are estimated to be 60% of total production costs [13].

      For swine flu vaccine, a news article [9] published in June (2010) shows that 40 million doses cost the government about $261 million – an average of $6.5/dose. Manufacturers’ selling price of a dose to private sectors is around $10-12 dollars ([3], [13]). So in general, the manufacturers sell flu vaccines to private sectors at a higher price than to government.

    • Profitability

      By US Census reports, for NAICS industry “Vaccines, toxoids, and antigents, for human use”, the 2002 percentage surplus of sales revenue over materials purchases and total payroll expenses, including fringe benefit costs, was 56.3% [28]. This price-cost margin is an estimate of the cash available after paying production costs to cover fixed plant, R&D, and corporate overhead costs. As a comparison, the price-cost margin for the pharmaceutical preparations industry is 62.3%. However, compared to the blockbuster drugs, vaccine markets offer limited sales potential as they are administered to a patient only once or a few times in a year.


    • Salvage value of excessive inventory


      Since the vaccine life-cycle is only one season (for both north and south hemispheres), all the leftover vaccines are tossed away at a loss for the manufacturers [18].

    • The new cell-based manufacturing technology

      The current flu vaccine production relying on growing viruses in chicken eggs is a decades-old technology. The next-generation cell-culture flu vaccine technology is currently under development. Cell-culture technology means that the flu viruses are grown in living cells – often in mammalian kidney cells. The technology has already been used to produce vaccines against hepatitis, chickenpox and shingles. The major advantage of this technology is that it is feasible to produce flu vaccines within six months of a pandemic. However, this technology currently has much lower yield and higher cost than the egg-based technology. For instance, a typical cell- based facility costs as much as $600 million and would only be able to produce about 40~50 million doses a year ([3], [13]). On the other hand, to build a new egg-based plant, it costs

      $150~$300 million for around $100 million doses [27].

  7. Industry Dynamics

    • Manufacturers’ market shares and dynamics

      All these years have witnessed a declining number of producers and products for key pediatric vaccines in general and flu vaccines in particular in the U.S. and other parts of the world. In 1967, there were 26 licensed manufacturers of such vaccines; in 2002, there were only 12 [12]. While remaining small, the number of flu vaccine manufacturers is increasing in recent years. See Exhibit 9 for the number of flu vaccine manufacturers in recent years ([3], [15], [30], CDC website).

    • Barrier of entry to the U.S. market

      Influenza vaccine manufacturers rarely worry about the threat of competition from new firms because the industry tends to be characterized by significant barriers to entry including significant government regulation (approval and compliance), large capital expenditure and long time needed for production facility (Section 6), and the production risks (detailed in Section 5). Although the clinical trials for flu vaccines may be simpler and take less time than other drugs [3], firms must demonstrate the capacity to produce vaccine before the FDA grants a production license. Even if a license is granted, the company must follow the FDA compliance of good manufacturing practice.

      While many drugs are sold in most major markets of the world, vaccine markets are largely regional and even country-specific because of the high fixed investment upfront and a relatively concentrated market where government plays a key role in the market and sets reference price. As a result, the U.S. only has a few approved flu vaccine manufacturers, while there are about 16 manufacturers of flu vaccines worldwide. For instance, Solvay, one of the EU’s largest suppliers of flu vaccines, does not have a product approved in the U.S. [12].

    • Impact of the limited number

      For the U.S., the lack of competition leads to conservative production of each manufacturer and then relatively small overall supply for the U.S. market. Because of the long time required to produce influenza vaccines, the loss of one manufacturer’s production can rarely be made up by other manufacturers during the same influenza season, which directly results in shortage.

  8. Distribution

    • The material flow

      The influenza vaccine distribution is divided into two channels. In one channel, vaccine doses are sold directly to customers (retail outlets, hospitals, mass immunizers, public health, physicians/long-term care, etc) and in the other channel vaccine doses are distributed through distributors to customers. Overall, manufacturers supply 50 % of total vaccine supply through each channel [15]. See Exhibit 10 for a complete picture.

      Manufacturers consider a number of factors when deciding whether to sell their product directly to health care providers or via wholesalers, primarily customer satisfaction (especially the priority groups), inventory availability and cost-effectiveness because influenza vaccine is a low cost product. Once the vaccine is distributed, it is extremely difficult to know where it is going due to the extreme fragmentation of the channel. This is relevant because in times of vaccine shortage or delay – whether nationally or in specific regions – the ability to redistribute vaccine relies on knowing where vaccine is located.

      Each vaccine manufacturer has its own method for distributing vaccines to purchasers and health care providers. Among TIVs manufacturers, Sanofi Pasteur sells the large majority of its influenza vaccine directly to purchasers and health care providers. The one wholesaler with whom they work is contractually obligated to carry no other manufacturers’ product.

    • Pre-booking


      Influenza manufacturers calculate the amount to be produced each year based on a process known as prebooking, in which buyers reserve what they intend to purchase in advance. The information collected in the prebooking process is essentially client lists. In times of shortage, manufacturers may elect to sell their influenza vaccine to whomever they choose.


    • Flu vaccination outlet types


      Physician offices and clinics are the primary sites for seasonal flu vaccine sales. According to the data available from the CDC, about 34% of vaccine produced in 2007-2008 (including that sold directly and through distributors) was sold to physician offices, 17.6% went to state and local

      health departments, 16.9 were sold to hospitals, and 19.1% went to pharmacies and secondary distributors. Less than 20% of flu vaccine sales were made to government agencies [15].


    • Weekly release


      Demand for flu vaccines typically starts in early August, then reaches it peak in October. Incidences of seasonal influenza historically peak in February [15], see Exhibit 11 for the cumulative vaccine releases.


    • Distributors’ cost structure, wholesale/retail pricing


      Distributors’ cost is essentially the manufacturers’ selling price per dose – $10-11. Their selling prices range from $15 to $30. Distributors can adjust orders in the season. Retail pricing depends on the outlet; for a pharmacy, it is about $25-40; for a doctor’s office: $15-50; Senior center:

      $15-30; Low cost clinic: $20. [Source : http://www.wisebread.com/how-much-does-a-flu-shot- cost].


      From the patient’s perspective, the price of getting a flu vaccine shot may be as little as $15, however, s/he may also have to pay the cost of administration, such as seeing a doctor.


    • Distributor/retailers’ salvage value


      Because unused influenza vaccine generally cannot be returned to the manufacturer once purchased, the health care providers or wholesalers that have vaccine at the end of the influenza season may suffer a financial loss.

  9. The Role of Government

    • The role of government

      Government plays a key role in the flu vaccine value chain: (1) conducting all research development activities involved with flu vaccines- starting with identification of the virus strain that will infect human population in the coming season; next predicting how widely spread the flu epidemic will be, and lastly by providing manufacturers with the virus strain seeds to produce vaccines (CDC). (2) Supervising manufacturers’ R&D and production processes (FDA – CBER).

      (3) Establishing a price reference for vaccine doses even though they secure less than 20% of the total US vaccine supply. In 2009, the government negotiated to pay $9-10 for a single vial of flu vaccine (Fluzone). (4) Working with private sectors to determine the priority groups. (5) Providing low-cost vaccines as a public service. (6) Subsidizing the cost of building a new

      production facility and renovating an existing facility and also granting awards to work on production process improvements.

    • Align manufacturers’ interest

      The U.S. government has generally been reluctant to pay companies to produce extra vaccines and create a reserve capacity. There are too many wasted flu vaccines when the flu season isn't severe and people choose not to get vaccinated. Vaccines are tossed away at a loss for the corporations [31].

      Some experts suggest the U.S. government should promise to purchase a fixed amount of flu vaccine – despite the cost and the likelihood that some of the money would end up being wasted (e.g., for swine flu vaccine, $261 million investment was written off as of July 1st 2010). Canada, for instance, has contracts with vaccine makers to cover most of its population. The largest contract is with ID Biomedical Corp. (recently acquired by GlaxoSmithKline PLC) for about eight million flu vaccine doses per year. That takes much of the risk out of the company's business, but still lets it manufacture additional doses for the private market or for other needy buyers [31].

      Other experts suggest that the U.S. government should share the cost of production for flu vaccines, or simply buy-out manufacturers’ left-over inventory at the end of each season. In this way, the government can provide the incentive to the manufacturer to overcome the production/demand risk and produce more so that the society can be better prepared for a pandemic influenza.

  10. Call for Action


Looking at the historical data, Michael finds that CSP’s market share, although varying over time, is around 37-55% in the last few years. Although the overall market size has grown in response to public policies, it still fluctuates from year to year, depending on many factors that are difficult to predict and almost impossible to control. As a matter of fact, no one can predict when outbreaks will occur in an influenza season, or how severe these outbreaks will be. Nevertheless, Michael must come up with a reasonable estimate of all possible demand scenarios and suggest a production plan that is in the best interest of the company and promote public health.


A simple way to do the job is to adopt the just-in-time strategy – eyeballing how much they sold last year, and perhaps increase it by 3~5% to be conservative. Anyway, flu vaccines are sold at very low prices. After a more careful look at the numbers, Michael found that in the last few

years, less than 60% of the recommended population was actually vaccinated. Quite puzzling, despite the reality of repeated shortages, millions of doses are wasted each year.


Such a just-in-time strategy, while being in the company’s best interest, is in the opposite direction to promoting public health and guarding against flu pandemic. If a pandemic is to come, the demand will be much higher and since manufacturers cannot resupply in six months, many people may suffer due to a severe flu vaccine shortage. Thus, for the interest of the society, a just-in-case strategy that prepares for the worst case may be necessary. However, the chance of a flu pandemic is small, and more importantly, as his boss cautioned,


“just like you can't plan for a surge capacity in an emergency room of 500 or 1,000 patients from the 20 you see in a day, you can't have 100 million doses of vaccine supply sitting around waiting for something to happen.” “It’s kind of odd that the government would expect all those years for us to continue to throw millions of doses of vaccine away every year and never take a hit on it.”[19] “We are a for-profit, publicly traded company. What I like to tell people is that neither Santa Claus nor UNICEF is listed on the NYSE.”[16]


Michael feels himself being torn apart by the “just-in-time” strategy that is in the company’s interest versus the “just-in-case” strategy that is in the interest of the public.
